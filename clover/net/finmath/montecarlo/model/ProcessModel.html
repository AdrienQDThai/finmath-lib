16    /**
17    * The interface for a model of a stochastic process <i>X</i> where
18    * <i>X(t) = f(t,Y(t))</i> and <br>
19    * \[
20    * dY_{j} = \mu_{j} dt + \lambda_{1,j} dW_{1} + \ldots + \lambda_{m,j} dW_{m}
21    * \]
22    *
23    * <ul>
24    * <li>The value of <i>Y(0)</i> is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#getInitialState}.
25    * <li>The value of &mu; is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#getDrift}.
26    * <li>The value &lambda;<sub>j</sub> is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#getFactorLoading}.
27    * <li>The function <i>f</i> is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#applyStateSpaceTransform}.
28    * </ul>
29    * Here, &mu; and &lambda;<sub>j</sub> may depend on <i>X</i>, which allows to implement stochastic drifts (like in a LIBOR market model)
30    * of local volatility models.
31    *
32    * <br>
33    * Examples:
34    * <ul>
35    * <li>
36    * The Black Scholes model can be modeled by S = X = Y (i.e. f is the identity)
37    * and &mu;<sub>1</sub> = r S and &lambda;<sub>1,1</sub> = &sigma; S.
38    * </li>
39    * <li>
40    * Alternatively, the Black Scholes model can be modeled by S = X = exp(Y) (i.e. f is exp)
41    * and &mu;<sub>1</sub> = r - 0.5 &sigma; &sigma; and &lambda;<sub>1,1</sub> = &sigma;.
42    * </li>
43    * </ul>
44    *
45    * @author Christian Fries
46    * @version 1.0
47    */
